Navigating Privacy in Biomedical Research and Open Science

The purpose of this resource is to help researchers navigate issues of privacy in biomedical research studies that aim to include open science practices.

Recommended Resources

This website is a curated collection of resources related privacy and sharing in health research, intended to be useful for someone trying to design an open science themed biomedical research study. I asked several thought leaders to recommend a few of their favorite resoures and to include at least one item from their own relevant work. The end result is a curated educational resource, organized by expert or by topic. Thanks to all the contributors!

Fun Facts

  • Fun Facts: A recent study exploring Parkinson's disease gave participant two choices for sharing their data, either (a) share data only with the research team or (b) share data with ALL qualified researchers worldwide. Out of the first ~16,000 enrolled participants, >60% chose to share their data with ALL researchers worldwide. Wow. The ongoing statistics around sharing are published by Sage BioNetworks. It will be interesting to see if the trend toward broad sharing holds over time. A report by the Health Data Exploration project showed that sharing preferences often depends on the type data (GPS, genetic, activity, etc).

Nomenclature

  • Equal Access: It is incredibly odd to me that most of the biomedical research enterprise operates with the expectation that people should altruistically give up their privacy around data that they themselves don't have access to. There is a relatively small (but growing!) band of researchers who are trying an approach that is new and different: give the participants acccess to their own slice of data, if they want to inspect it. The idea that participants in research are given rights to access data about them being held by the researchers is what I call "equal access." My feeling is that sometimes good ideas don't catch on until we have good terminology. I hope this idea, if not the term, catches on.
