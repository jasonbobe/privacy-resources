Navigating Privacy in Biomedical Research and Open Science

The purpose of this resource is to help researchers navigate issues of privacy in biomedical research studies that aim to include open science practices.

About

This website is a curated collection of resources related privacy and sharing in health research. Our aim here is to provide useful information for someone trying to include open science practices in the design of a biomedical research study that involves personal health information collected from people. I asked several thought leaders to recommend a few of their favorite resoures and to include at least one item from their own relevant work. The end result will be a curated educational resource, organized by contributor and by topic.

Jason Bobe, Sharing Lab, Icahn Institute at Mount Sinai

  • Educational Guide:
    1. Future Privacy Forum's new Visual Guide to Practical Data De-Identification. This is a great representation of the continuum of de-identification, an important concept to understand especially in the context of legal requirements in the United States, which as Mark Rothstein points out, are bimodal: "If the information is 'identifiable,' then all of the legal protections are applicable; if the information is 'not identifiable,' then there are no protections whatsoever".
  • Fun Facts:
    1. A recent study exploring Parkinson's disease gave participant two choices for sharing their data, either (a) share data only with the research team or (b) share data with ALL qualified researchers worldwide. Out of the first ~16,000 enrolled participants, >60% chose to share their data with ALL researchers worldwide. Wow. The ongoing statistics around sharing are published by Sage BioNetworks. It will be interesting to see if the trend toward broad sharing holds over time. A report by the Health Data Exploration project showed that sharing preferences often depends on the type data (GPS, genetic, activity, etc).
  • Debates:
    1. Generally, I am in the same camp as people when they say that they should "own their data" much like U.S. President Barack Obama said at one of the Precision Medicine Initiative events. However, as legal scholar Barbara Evans points out, treating data like property is not as legally sound as you might think, even if it is a seemingly nifty mental model for expressing the desire for some control over one's own personal data.
  • Toolkits:
    1. John Wilbanks has been innovating in the backwater of research governance that is informed consent. The consent form, as John likes to say, is "written by a doctor, edited by a lawyer, and approved by a committee" (as such, doomed to mediocrity). The participant has gotten short shrift, until now. Check out his Participant-Centered Consent Toolkit and find some great ways to instantly improve the process of informed consent in your study.
  • Nomenclature:
    1. It is incredibly odd to me that most of the biomedical research enterprise operates with the expectation that people should altruistically give up their privacy around data that they themselves don't have access to. There is a relatively small (but growing!) band of researchers who are trying a different approach: give the participants acccess to their own slice of data. The idea that participants in research are given rights to access data about them being held by the researchers is what I call "equal access." My feeling is that sometimes good ideas don't catch on until we have good terminology. I hope this idea, if not the term, catches on.
  • Classic Cases:
    1. Danish researchers publicly released data from dating site, OK Cupid, without permission from users. An instant classic of how not to behave as a researcher. See summary of the case in the Daily Dot
    2. Typically, re-identification is about the threat of third parties being able to identify anonymous participants. In one recent case, a participant was able to identify a sketch of her own profile while reading a news article about a research study she volunteered for.

    1. Many people want to share their data to support research, to help other people, or contribute to the common good in other ways. In such cases, data sharing is an enactment of solidarity. We developed a governance framework that is particularly suited to cases of data sharing motivated by solidarity. Key elements are a consent process where potential data sharers learn about the mission and values of the organisation that uses their data (or facilitates data sharing) and where there is transparency also about commercial stakes and interests. Other elements are that people retain access to their own data, and have access to findings. The solidarity-based approach also envisages the establishment of harm mitigation funds in cases where people who share data solidaristically could be harmed by the use of these data. Further details on solidarity-based data governance can be found here. . Or get in touch with us for practical help and updates! barbara.prainsack@kcl.ac.uk

  • Published papers:

  • Legal
    1. The public controversy over Facebook’s secret research spilled into the policy sphere: Is consent and IRB review the right model? Many perspectives were captured in this symposium issue of the Colorado Technology Law Journal. And my own perspective, which is highly skeptical of both consent and IRB review (favoring risk-based regulation of research instead), is available here.
  • Debates:
    1. As for the debate about reidentification risk and open data, in addition to the excellent resources provided by others, I recommend Felix Wu’s work on the subject, Defining Privacy and Utility in Data Sets, and Khaled El Emam et al., A Systematic Review of Re-Identification Attacks on Health Data.

      When data has been deidentified, reidentification attacks can be overwhelmed with false matches—a fact that is often overlooked in the popular press. Three examples are available here (matching attacks correct at most 47% of the time), here (matching attacks correct 5-63% of the time), and here (only two out of twenty-eight reidentifications were correct).

      The sense of risk is also exaggerated when uniqueness within a deidentified database is used as an estimate for reidentification risk, and when bad anonymization techniques are used as a representation of all anonymization techniques. Compare this paper published in Science (concluding that credit card data cannot be safely anonymized) to this critique, also published in Science (pointing out the uniqueness and straw man problems).

      Full disclosure: I am a partisan in this debate. I have argued that the moral imperative points toward, rather than away from, publicly accessible deidentified databases in Tragedy of the Data Commons. In more recent experimental work, I found that consumers do not seem to care about reidentification risk (whereas they do care about the sale of data to pharmaceutical companies).

Lauren Smith, Future Privacy Forum

  • Guide:
    1. Recognizing the need for strong Best Practices, FPF released Best Practices for Consumer Wearables and Wellness Apps and Devices (PDF), a detailed set of guidelines that responsible companies can follow to ensure they provide practical privacy protections for consumer-generated health and wellness data. The document was produced with support from the Robert Wood Johnson Foundation and incorporates input from a wide range of stakeholders including companies, advocates, and regulators.
  • Legal:
    1. Report from the Office of the National Coordinator for Health Information Technology (ONC) issued to Congress entitled, Examining Oversight of the Privacy & Security of Health Data Collected by Entities Not Regulated by HIPAA. This report—developed in coordination with the Office for Civil Rights (OCR) and the U.S. Federal Trade Commission (FTC)—discusses the lack of clear guidance around consumer access to, and privacy and security of, health information collected, shared, and used by entities that are not currently covered by HIPAA.
  • Fun Facts:
    1. A new FPF Mobile Apps Study underscores the necessity of strong Best Practices for health and wellness data. The App Study revealed that while the number of apps that provide privacy policies continues its upward trend from our previous surveys in 2011 and 2012, health and fitness apps – which often control and link to wearable devices, and which can collect sensitive health and wellness data – do worse than average at providing privacy policies. Only 70% of top health and fitness apps had a privacy policy (6% lower than overall top apps), and only 61% linked to it from the app store listing page (10% lower than overall top apps).
